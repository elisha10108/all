About an entire village

Until 1948, part of the Arab village of Salma existed on the site of the neighborhood. This village had in 1931 3,691 inhabitants and about 800 houses. On the eve of the War of Independence, the village had about 7,600 residents,

most of whom were residents from the area who moved to live in this village, which is considered the center of the Arab population. Among the residents were also wealthy people from Jaffa who built village houses there. From the village, snipers fired at the neighboring Jewish neighborhoods

(the Hatikva neighborhood, the Ezra neighborhood and Yad Eliyahu). During Operation Chametz, which took place on April 22, 1948, the village of Selma was captured, and its residents fled to Jaffa. Following the declaration of independence of the State of Israel, the town's residents were settled by Jewish refugees from the area and new immigrants, mainly families of Yemeni immigrants,

who arrived in an operation on eagles' wings. Later, barracks and temporary structures were also erected to accommodate additional immigrants. The area of ​​the village is divided into three areas - Selma A, B and C. In November 1948, most of the village's areas were annexed to Tel Aviv and about a third of them (Selma III) to Ramat Later, the name of the village was changed from "Salma" to "Kfar Shalem", which was now a very crowded Tel Aviv neighborhood - with about 20,000 inhabitants. Apart from the houses of the Arab village, the neighborhoods of Ramat Hen, Givat Geula, Ramat HaShikma, the Bulgariim neighborhood, the Hefetz Haim neighborhood and the Ramat Gan National Park were built on the land of the village.

The state sought to demolish the village houses and build new houses. In 1965, the Construction Evacuation Law was enacted and a process of evacuating the residents from the neighborhood began. This process has known ups and downs in the issue of resident-establishment ratio; One of the most serious quarrels occurred in December 1982, when a resident of the neighborhood, Shimon Yehoshua, tried to prevent the demolition of his house and was shot dead by a police officer. In the early 1990s, hundreds of families receiving compensation were evacuated. Many of them received housing in high-rise housing in the area, which soon became, due to housing overcrowding, what residents call "slams on pillars." For some of the families, the Tel Aviv-Yafo municipality allowed them to continue living in ground-level buildings. All two families were given a half-dunam plot, and permission to build a two-family house. Due to the rarity of detached houses, and the population exchange policy in Tel Aviv, the Tel Aviv-Yafo Municipality has decided that all detached houses in Tel Aviv will pay a uniform property tax, regardless of the other services provided there. About 300 families that were not included in the arrangements of the 1990s, now live in complexes destined for demolition. Against 30 families out of the approximately 300 families living in the complexes to be demolished, an eviction order was issued without compensation from the residents, as it was alleged that the lands on which the residents' homes stand do not belong to the state (under guardianship of absentee property), but are privately owned. It was argued that since the lands belonged in 1948 to a British investor who was not considered absent, they were not expropriated by the state, they are not owned by her and therefore there is no obligation to compensate the residents. The Supreme Court accepted this claim, and the eviction by force took place on December 25, 2007. Activists and social organizations criticized this ruling on the basis of the claim of distributive justice and ethno-class inequality. Thus, for example, the lawyer Dr. Sandy Kedar argued that although it is possible that in the narrow legal aspect the Supreme Court was right in its ruling, "if you look at what happens in an entire village compared, for example, to Kibbutz Galil Yam, it can be argued that it is a discriminatory policy bordering on illegality ".
